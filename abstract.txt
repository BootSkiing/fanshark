# Abstract
Your AI server is leaking its own brain, and you can hear it. This talk introduces GLARE (Gradient-Latent Acoustic Resonance Exfiltration): an attack that recovers a model's secret weights by listening to its cooling fans while it runs inference.
Every forward pass heats the GPU in a predictable pattern, and the fans re-emit that pattern as a faint hum recoverable from across the room. Prior side-channel work has focused on power draw and EM emissions; GLARE is the first to treat the airflow subsystem itself as the oracle. Building on CVE-2025-88213 and RFC 9981 ("Thermal Framing for Accelerated Compute"), our tool fanshark (github.com/bootkskiing/fanshark, 2.3k stars) turns the hum back into usable weights with 91.7% accuracy in under nine minutes.
We tested GLARE against 1,204 production deployments; it worked on 73.4% of them. Air-gapped servers were worse off, because their sealed enclosures act as passive resonators.
Findings were disclosed to affected vendors under a 90-day timeline. We close with the bad news about fixes: fan jitter and acoustic foam mostly fail, and disabling the fans trades one problem for CVE-2026-01472 (thermal denial-of-inference).
